Understanding CRMicro IGBT Technology and Applications

Comprehensive guide to IGBT technology, characteristics, and applications

Understanding CRMicro IGBT Technology and Applications

By Mike Roberts, Application Engineer

January 15, 2025

Reading time: 12 min

Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistors (IGBTs) represent a critical technology in power electronics, combining the high input impedance of MOSFETs with the low conduction losses of bipolar transistors. CRMicro's IGBT portfolio leverages advanced trench and field-stop technologies to deliver superior performance across various power conversion applications.

IGBT Structure and Operation

An IGBT is a three-terminal power semiconductor device that functions as an electronic switch. It combines an isolated gate FET for control input and a bipolar power transistor as the output stage. The gate terminal controls the device similar to a MOSFET, while the collector and emitter terminals behave like a bipolar transistor.

During turn-on, a voltage applied to the gate terminal creates a conductive channel in the MOSFET portion, allowing current to flow from the emitter to the collector through the N-drift region. This current injection modulates the conductivity of the drift region, reducing voltage drop across the device.

The IGBT's structure includes several key regions:

  • P+ collector - Injects holes into the drift region
  • N-drift region - Determines voltage blocking capability
  • P-base region - Forms the channel for gate control
  • N+ source - Provides low-resistance contact

IGBT Characteristics

Voltage-Current Characteristics

IGBTs exhibit a saturation region similar to bipolar transistors. The collector-emitter saturation voltage (VCE(sat)) is typically lower than a MOSFET's RDS(on) × ID, making IGBTs more suitable for high-current applications. However, VCE(sat) has a positive temperature coefficient, which is beneficial for parallel operation.

Switching Characteristics

IGBT switching involves four phases:

  1. Turn-on delay - Gate charges to threshold voltage
  2. Rise time - Collector voltage drops as current rises
  3. Turn-off delay - Gate discharges below threshold
  4. Fall time - Current falls to zero while voltage rises

CRMicro's advanced IGBTs utilize field-stop technology to reduce tail current during turn-off, improving switching performance.

IGBT Technology Generations

First Generation (Planar Gate)

Early IGBTs used a planar gate structure with relatively high VCE(sat) and slow switching. These devices were primarily used in low-frequency applications where conduction losses dominated.

Second Generation (Trench Gate)

Trench gate IGBTs improved the trade-off between VCE(sat) and switching speed. The vertical trench structure reduced cell pitch and improved current density.

Third Generation (Field-Stop)

Field-stop technology reduced the thickness of the N-drift region, lowering both VCE(sat) and turn-off losses. CRMicro's current portfolio includes third-generation field-stop devices.

Fourth Generation (Advanced Field-Stop)

Latest generation devices optimize the trade-off between conduction and switching losses while improving short-circuit ruggedness. These devices are ideal for high-frequency industrial applications.

IGBT vs. MOSFET Selection Guide

Choosing between IGBTs and MOSFETs depends on the application requirements:

IGBT Advantages

  • Lower conduction losses at high voltages (>1000V) and currents
  • Better performance in hard-switching applications
  • More favorable safety margin in short-circuit conditions
  • Less temperature sensitivity in conduction losses

MOSFET Advantages

  • Higher switching frequencies (>100kHz)
  • Lower gate drive power
  • No tail current during turn-off
  • Proportional VCE(sat) to RDS(on) × ID for paralleling

General selection rule:

MOSFETs are preferred for voltages below 1000V and switching frequencies above 50kHz. IGBTs are preferred for voltages above 1000V and switching frequencies below 20kHz. In the mid-range (600V-1200V and 20kHz-50kHz), the choice depends on current density and specific application requirements.

Key Application Areas

Motor Drives

IGBTs are the dominant technology in variable frequency drives (VFDs) and servo systems. CRMicro's IGBTs are optimized for the typical switching frequencies (2-16 kHz) and load conditions in motor drive applications.

Key considerations in motor drive applications:

  • Short-circuit capability for fault protection
  • Temperature stability for consistent operation
  • Low tail current to minimize switching losses
  • Body diode reverse recovery characteristics

Industrial Power Supplies

IGBTs are ideal for high-power switching power supplies, particularly in applications requiring 1-20kW power levels. They offer optimal performance in off-line converters, forward converters, and resonant topologies.

Solar Inverters

CRMicro IGBTs are optimized for various solar inverter topologies including string inverters and central inverters. The field-stop technology is particularly beneficial for the switching frequencies (10-20kHz) typical in PV applications.

Gate Drive Requirements

Proper gate drive is critical for IGBT performance:

Gate Voltage Levels

IGBTs typically require +15V for turn-on and 0V (or slightly negative) for turn-off. Gate drive circuits should provide adequate current for fast turn-on/off while avoiding gate oscillations.

Gate Resistance

Gate resistance (Rg) affects switching speed:

  • Lower Rg = faster switching, more EMI
  • Higher Rg = slower switching, lower EMI, higher switching losses

CRMicro recommends different gate resistances based on voltage class and application requirements.

Miller Effect Considerations

IGBTs are susceptible to the Miller effect, where the collector-emitter voltage change during switching induces current through the Miller capacitance (Cres), potentially causing unintended turn-on. Proper gate drive design must account for this effect.

Thermal Considerations

IGBTs operate at higher temperatures than MOSFETs, with maximum junction temperatures often reaching 150°C. However, VCE(sat) has a positive temperature coefficient, which provides current sharing stability when devices are paralleled.

Thermal design for IGBT applications should consider:

  • Combined conduction and switching losses
  • Thermal cycling for application lifetime
  • Thermal interface materials
  • Heatsink design for the power dissipation

Protection and Safety

Short Circuit Protection

IGBTs can survive short circuit conditions for a limited time (typically 1-10 microseconds). Designers should implement fast-acting protection circuits that detect overcurrent conditions and safely turn off the device before thermal damage occurs.

Overtemperature Protection

Internal temperature sensors or external thermal monitoring should detect overheating conditions and reduce power or shut down the system.

Undervoltage Lockout

Gate drive circuits should prevent IGBT turn-on when gate supply voltage is insufficient, preventing device damage from partial turn-on.

Emerging IGBT Technologies

CRMicro continues to invest in IGBT technology development:

SiC-Based IGBTs

Combining IGBT structure with SiC material properties could provide a path beyond current silicon limitations, though commercial devices are still under development.

Advanced Cell Structures

Future IGBT generations will feature more sophisticated cell designs to further optimize the conduction-switching loss trade-off.

Conclusion

IGBTs remain a vital technology in power electronics, providing optimal performance in medium to high power applications where MOSFETs and thyristors are not suitable. CRMicro's IGBT portfolio offers advanced field-stop technology optimized for various applications.

Successful IGBT implementation requires careful attention to gate drive, thermal management, and protection circuits. When selecting IGBTs for your application, consider voltage, current, frequency, and environmental requirements.
