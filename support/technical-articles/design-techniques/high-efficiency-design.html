Power Electronics Design: CRMicro Solutions for High-Efficiency Applications

By Jane Smith, M.Eng., Senior Application Engineer

Reading time: 14 min

Power electronics efficiency has become increasingly critical in modern applications, driven by environmental regulations, sustainability initiatives, and cost-saving demands. CRMicro's comprehensive portfolio of power semiconductors enables designers to achieve exceptional efficiency across various power conversion applications. This guide explores techniques for maximizing efficiency using CRMicro components.

Fundamentals of Power Conversion Efficiency

Efficiency (η) in power conversion is defined as:

η = POUT / PIN = (PIN - PLOSS) / PIN

Total power losses in power conversion systems comprise multiple sources:

  1. Conduction losses - Resistive losses in power semiconductors and passive components
  2. Switching losses - Energy lost during turn-on and turn-off transitions
  3. Magnetic losses - Hysteresis and eddy current losses in magnetic components
  4. Capacitive losses - Dielectric losses in capacitors
  5. Gate drive losses - Power consumed by driving gates of FETs and IGBTs

Minimizing these losses requires careful component selection, optimal design techniques, and understanding of device characteristics.

CRMicro Component Advantages for High Efficiency

CRMicro's power semiconductor portfolio is engineered to maximize efficiency across various applications:

Low RDS(on) MOSFETs

CRMicro's SGT (Shielded Gate Trench) and SJ (Super Junction) MOSFET technologies achieve industry-leading figures of merit (FOM):

  • SGT MOSFETs: Lowest RDS(on) × Qg for low-voltage applications (12V-200V)
  • SJ MOSFETs: Optimal RDS(on) × Eoss for high-voltage applications (600V-800V), ideal for PFC and adapter applications

CRMicro's 650V SJ MOSFETs offer RDS(on) values down to 65mΩ in TO-247 packages, enabling conduction losses of less than 1W in 2kW PFC applications.

Advanced IGBT Technology

CRMicro IGBTs incorporate field-stop technology to optimize the conduction-switching loss trade-off:

  • Lower VCE(sat) for reduced conduction losses
  • Optimized tail current for reduced turn-off losses
  • Improved short-circuit ruggedness for reliable operation

Wide Bandgap Solutions

CRMicro's SiC and GaN devices offer significant efficiency advantages:

  • SiC MOSFETs: Zero recovery charge, enabling higher frequency operation with reduced switching losses
  • SiC Schottky Diodes: Zero recovery charge, eliminating reverse recovery losses in various topologies
  • GaN HEMTs: Ultra-low output capacitance, ideal for high-frequency applications

Topology Selection for Maximum Efficiency

Power Factor Correction (PFC)

For universal input PFC applications:

  • Continuous Conduction Mode (CCM) using CRMicro 650V SJ MOSFETs offers excellent efficiency at moderate power levels (200W-3kW)
  • Critical Conduction Mode (CrCM) using CRMicro SGT MOSFETs provides high efficiency at light loads
  • Interleaved PFC using multiple CRMicro devices reduces ripple current and improves efficiency

DC-DC Conversion

For isolated DC-DC applications:

  • Forward converters using CRMicro MOSFETs for low to medium power applications (50W-300W)
  • Half-bridge topology using CRMicro IGBTs for higher power levels (500W-5kW)
  • LLC resonant converters using CRMicro SiC MOSFETs for zero-voltage switching (ZVS) operation
  • Phase-shift full-bridge using CRMicro IGBTs for high-power applications

Non-Isolated DC-DC

For point-of-load applications:

  • Synchronous buck using CRMicro SGT MOSFETs for high-efficiency conversion at low voltages
  • Multi-phase operation using CRMicro small-package MOSFETs for high-current applications

Design Techniques for Efficiency Optimization

Minimizing Conduction Losses

Conduction losses dominate in low-frequency applications and can be minimized through:

  • Device selection: Choose lowest RDS(on) or VCE(sat) within package and cost constraints
  • Parallel operation: Paralleling CRMicro devices reduces effective RDS(on) (MOSFETs) or VCE(sat) (IGBTs)
  • Package selection: TO-247 and D2PAK offer lowest thermal resistance to reduce temperature-related increases in conduction losses
  • Thermal management: Effective heatsinking keeps devices cool, maintaining low on-resistance

Minimizing Switching Losses

Switching losses become more significant at higher frequencies and can be reduced by:

  • ZVS/ZCS techniques: Implement soft-switching topologies using CRMicro SiC devices to virtually eliminate switching losses
  • Optimized gate drive: Proper gate resistor selection to minimize switching time while controlling ringing
  • Dead time optimization: Minimizing body diode conduction time in synchronous topologies using CRMicro low-Qrr devices
  • Frequency selection: Balancing switching and magnetic losses for optimal efficiency at specific operating points

Minimizing Magnetic Losses

Although related to external components, magnetic losses can be minimized through:

  • Frequency optimization: Higher frequencies allow smaller magnetics but increase core losses
  • Waveform shaping: Using CRMicro devices to shape current waveforms and reduce RMS values
  • Interleaving: Multiple-phase operation using CRMicro devices reduces ripple current and magnetic losses

Efficiency Measurement and Verification

Accurate efficiency measurement is crucial for validating design optimizations:

Measurement Setup

For accurate efficiency measurements:

  • Use calibrated power analyzers for input and output power measurement
  • Account for measurement uncertainty in accuracy calculations
  • Test at various load conditions (10%, 25%, 50%, 75%, 100%)
  • Verify performance at worst-case temperature conditions

Loss Breakdown Analysis

Understanding loss distribution helps identify improvement opportunities:

  • Separate conduction and switching losses by varying frequency
  • Measure individual component losses where possible
  • Use thermal imaging to identify hotspots

Advanced Efficiency Techniques

Adaptive Dead Time Control

Implementing adaptive dead time using CRMicro device characteristics:

  • Dynamically adjust dead time based on load conditions
  • Minimize body diode conduction time without shoot-through risk
  • Use CRMicro devices with predictable threshold characteristics

Dynamic Voltage Scaling

Adjusting voltage levels based on load requirements:

  • Lower bus voltage at light loads to reduce switching losses
  • Utilize CRMicro devices with appropriate voltage ratings
  • Implement smooth transitions to avoid transients

Multi-Phase Interleaving

For high-current applications using CRMicro devices:

  • Reduce input/output ripple current
  • Distribute losses across multiple phases
  • Implement with CRMicro MOSFETs in small packages for compact design

EMI Considerations in High-Efficiency Designs

High-efficiency designs often use faster switching, which can increase EMI. Considerations include:

  • Soft switching: ZVS and ZCS techniques using CRMicro SiC devices reduce EMI compared to hard switching
  • Spread spectrum: Modulating switching frequency to spread EMI energy
  • GaN devices: CRMicro GaN devices offer extremely fast switching but require careful layout
  • Layout optimization: Minimize loop areas and use proper ground planes

Maximizing efficiency in power electronics applications requires a holistic approach combining optimal component selection, appropriate topology choice, and advanced design techniques.
