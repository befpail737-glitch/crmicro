Automotive Power Semiconductor Certification Trends

Industry Analysis Team September 28, 2025 Industry News

Automotive power semiconductors undergo rigorous testing to meet AEC-Q101 and other automotive standards

The automotive industry is experiencing increasingly stringent requirements for power semiconductor certification, driven by the rapid adoption of electric vehicles and advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS). This trend is significantly impacting design cycles, product development timelines, and supplier qualification processes.

Current Certification Standards

The primary certification standard for automotive power semiconductors is AEC-Q101, developed by the Automotive Electronics Council. This standard specifies stress test qualification requirements for discrete semiconductors. Key tests include:

  • Temperature cycling (thermal shock)
  • Highly Accelerated Stress Test (HAST)
  • Unclamped Inductive Switching (UIS)
  • Avalanche energy testing
  • Short circuit ruggedness
  • Electrostatic discharge (ESD) testing

Evolving Requirements

Recent changes to automotive certification requirements include:

Extended Temperature Ranges

Modern automotive power semiconductors must operate reliably over extended temperature ranges, from -40°C to +175°C junction temperature, to accommodate the demands of electrified vehicles with more challenging thermal environments.

Enhanced Reliability Testing

The introduction of ISO 26262 for functional safety has led to requirements for more comprehensive reliability testing, including accelerated aging tests that simulate the entire vehicle lifecycle under accelerated conditions.

SiC and GaN Specific Tests

With the growing adoption of silicon carbide (SiC) and gallium nitride (GaN) devices in automotive applications, new test methodologies are being developed to address the unique characteristics of wide bandgap semiconductors.

"The automotive qualification process for power semiconductors has become significantly more comprehensive over the past five years," said an industry analyst. "What used to take 6-9 months now often extends to 12-18 months, including additional tests required by specific automotive OEMs."

Impact on Design Cycles

The extended certification requirements are having several impacts on design cycles:

Longer Development Timelines

Power semiconductor development cycles have extended from 18-24 months to 24-36 months to accommodate the additional testing and validation required for automotive applications.

Increased Design Margins

Designers are incorporating additional margins to ensure devices pass all qualification tests, potentially sacrificing performance to achieve certification.

Early Qualification Planning

Manufacturers are beginning qualification planning much earlier in the design cycle, sometimes during the product definition phase, to reduce time-to-market.

Electric Vehicle Impact

The growth of electric vehicles is driving even more stringent requirements:

  • Higher reliability requirements due to safety-critical nature of EV powertrains
  • Extended lifetime requirements (15+ years vs. 10 years for conventional vehicles)
  • More rigorous thermal cycling due to battery pack thermal management
  • Higher voltage requirements (800V systems vs. 400V in earlier EVs)

OEM-Specific Requirements

Major automotive OEMs are implementing their own additional testing requirements beyond AEC-Q101:

Supplier Audits

Many OEMs now require on-site audits of semiconductor manufacturing facilities as part of the qualification process.

PPAP Process

The Production Part Approval Process (PPAP) requires detailed documentation of the manufacturing process, including statistical process control data.

Early Supplier Involvement

OEMs are involving semiconductor suppliers earlier in vehicle development projects, sometimes during concept phase, to ensure components meet automotive requirements.

Future Trends

Several trends are expected to shape future certification requirements:

AI and Autonomous Driving

As vehicles become more autonomous, power semiconductor reliability requirements will continue to increase. Level 5 autonomous vehicles will require semiconductors with extremely high reliability and failsafe operation.

Standardization Efforts

Industry groups are working to standardize certification procedures for wide bandgap semiconductors, which may eventually reduce the complexity of the qualification process.

Certification Harmonization

Efforts are underway to harmonize qualification requirements between different regions and OEMs, which could reduce redundant testing.

The evolving certification landscape represents both a challenge and an opportunity for power semiconductor manufacturers. Companies that invest early in automotive-qualified product development and maintain strong relationships with automotive customers are likely to benefit from the growing electrification trend.
